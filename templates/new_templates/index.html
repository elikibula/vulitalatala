Principal's Foreword

As Principal of the College, I take this time and space to welcome each and every member of the Davuilevu Theological College and the Methodist family at large. The College belongs to you, in as much as you belong to the college, that relationship and connection have proven stronger over the course of 116 years since the College moved from Navuloa to Davuilevu in 1908.

I took over the reign of leadership of the college after my predecessor the Rev. Dr. Jolami Lasawa ascends to the position as General Secretary of the Methodist Church in Fiji. I cannot thank him enough for the enormous effort he has shown over the years in directing this learning community in all aspects of college life. And I salute his family as well.

This academic year is a full house one in that all three streams of Diplomas [Years 1-3], and the Bachelor of degree Year Three, are part of the Academic Programme. More to that is the fact that DTC will be gearing up for the 2025 Accreditation Processes and the college will be preparing for this programme accreditation mandate this year. It implies that DTC is accredited by the Higher Education Commission Fiji [HECF], and the South Pacific Association of Theological Schools [SPATS]. Both these accrediting bodies have really lifted the quality and standard of Theological Education. This is a green light in that Theological Education here at DTC cannot really afford to be lagging behind, or remain complacent in terms of development, striving to be relevant, be influential, and be in par with the challenges and opportunities of a contemporary Fiji.

This year will mark the preparation for the launching of the Master’s Degree in 2025. It is a far cry from the years of Lth., Diploma, and BD as standard academic program here at DTC. PreparationS include designing new courses, designing pathways for the program, acquiring the services of brilliant mind in the field, uplifting of infrastructure, getting the program accredited by SPATS and HECF, and the financial side of this higher standard of academic learning. Shortly, DTC will witness the opening of the new Master’s Program building to coincide with the launching next year. That building will remain a living testimony to the financial contribution of present and past Ministers within the MCIF. And to add on to that, it showcases what Ministers can further contribute to the MCIF in terms of finances using the concept of ‘kacivi yaca’.

Ecumenism is very much alive in DTC with the inclusion of students from the Anglican Church. However, courses here at DTC are in themselves transcending the boundary of a ‘confined ecclesiological way of Theological thinking’, to a broader and more inclusive one for that matter. Arguably, courses are doing wonders in these aspects of academic journey. DTC intends to reaffirm that its roots are deeply entrenched in the soil of Methodism with newness, and freshness. The life and work of our current and future Ministers will be shaped, and molded by a sort of theology that is deeply stooped in the soil of Methodism in Fiji. That in-depth knowledge will direct the 2024 theme “Towards a Quality and Effective Theological Education”. And DTC will think deeply into achieving that theme. With certainty and hope, DTC will venture into the end of the year graduation in both programs of study. Wishing DTC a blessed Academic Year.


Rev. Savenaca Vuetanavanua

The competitive spirit within our human society is a major concern to our religious organizations, as we always predict and foresee the unity in diversity within the household of God. However, God has given us knowledge and insight to think outside the box and acquire what is relevant and appropriate for this modern age. The curriculum of the College should be reviewed in order to respond and counter the emergence of different facet of issues. It is appropriate to design the College’s curriculum as a reflection of the current challenges of the day. We cannot escape from the new challenges that are confronting us daily.

We need to merge or blend the religious perspectives and secular views of any emerging issues in context. In this regard, the language may somehow make the difference. Hopefully, its time to make a shift and move forward cautiously with courage and determination. Our ministers and deaconesses are challenged with issues such as: conflicts or tensions within the traditional leaderships; Church and Vanua relationship; Climate change; issues surrounding the ownership and utilization of resources; indiscipline and bad management of wealth and resources; rising poverty in the country; issues of diaspora and broken families; cyber-crime and drug addiction; violence against women and children and so forth. We need to re-design and re-focus our training programs to meet the demands of our different communities. May we continue to equip and send students with appropriate tools.

Rev. Dr. Semisi Turagavou

Historical Background of DTC

When the Wesleyan Methodist Missionaries arrived in 1835, serious consideration of introducing theological training in Fiji was at the forefront of their mission work. That dream was actualized when the first Theological College was built in Nasima, Taveuni, Cakaudrove in 1841. Rev. John Hunt was its first Principal when 8 were registered as students. The Fiji District Meeting in 1841 resolved to close the theological institution in Nasima in early 1842 due to the disagreement of its location.

They shifted the theological institute to Viwa, Bau in 1842 and Hunt was still its head. The institution was there until 1848. Eight registered students also registered for training. It was in Viwa on 4th October 1848, John Hunt, first theological institution Principal, was called to eternal rest. On the same year, the Fiji District Meeting agreed that the theological institution be shifted to Bucainabua in Lakeba. Rev. Richard Lyth was its Principal. The Theological College was in Lakeba from 1848 until it closed down in 1855.

After a year of confusion of where the institution to be shifted, the missionaries agreed that Mataisuva in Rewa be the appropriate sight. Rev. John Polglase was its Principal. It began in 1857 until 1860 when it was again closed. The Theological Institution moved to Kadavu in 1861 and located at Richmond, until 1872. Three staff members managed the institution. They were Reverends Jessey Carey, Joseph Nettleton, and Joseph Waterhouse.

From 1873, when Rev. W. A. Heighway was the Principal, the theological institute was shifted to Navuloa, the land belonged to the Naibati tribe in Buretu. The institution was there until 1907. Location continued to be a problem. In Navuloa, the Fiji District Meeting resolved after some serious discussions that the location of the institution be moved to Davuilevu as the Navuloa land is unproductive and swampy. Members of the District Meeting preferred that Davuilevu be the appropriate sight. In 1908, the final journey of the theological institution canoe landed at Davuilevu. The first Principal in Davuilevu was W. E. Bennett, the presiding Principal of Navuloa.

One of the purposes of shifting the Theological institute from Navuloa to Davuilevu was to let the Theological Institute closely follow the new developments the Government of Fiji has initiated for the development of this country. As the theological institute’s purpose is mission, it has to embrace its prophetic mission in a changing face of Fiji by encountering the negative side of civilization.

In addition to that, the Fiji Mission believed that the institute should move into the idea of enhancing quality theological training in a changed Fiji. Rev. Dr. Allan Tippet in 1960 paved the way forward of the idea when he partnered the Davuilevu Theological Institute with the Theological Institute in Melbourne, Australia. During that time, the Davuilevu Theological Institute awarded the Degree of LTh or Licentiate of Theology. After some year, the degree awarded became the Diploma in Theology. In 2003, the Bachelor of Divinity program started. Now the theological institute has moved on into the deeper water of enhancing theological training to its ordained ministry by introducing the program of Bachelor of Ministry in 2005. From Nasima in 1841 to Davuilevu in 1908, the theological institute embraces what the mission pioneers believed, that theological education and its mission is important in Fiji.

Our Vision

1.1 The College vision is: Effective and transforming ordained ministry for a successful society in a changing world through sound spiritual formation and development.

1.2 The College’s vision is rooted in the commitment by the Methodist Church to ensure its effective role in the future of Fiji, thus the institutionalized study of Methodist divinity in Fiji.

1.3 The vision stands alongside the Ministry of the Word, that is, the Old and New Testaments.

Our Mission

2.1 The College’s mission since the establishment of the first training institution is to train ministers of the Methodist Church in Fiji for ordination, as follows:

  1. (a) To prepare men and women fully for ordained ministry for the Methodist Church in Fiji through effective spiritual and theological training and development;
  2. (b) To encourage higher theological understanding and enhanced spirituality in an evolving society through a well-trained Christian workforce and leadership.
Our Objectives

3.1 To achieve its mission in the training and preparation of ministers, the College has the following four main objectives:

  1. (a) To direct a student to understand the vitality of spiritual and character formation, where his or her faith, repentance, freedom from sin, justification, renewal, sanctity, scriptural holiness, and being a child of God are of utmost priority;
  2. (b) To train and instil humility, piety, patience, diligence, and wisdom in students so they become successful Methodist Ministers;
  3. (c) To develop a student’s critical thinking relevant to the pursuit of academic excellence;
  4. (d) To instil, in a student, an earnest desire for knowledge and wisdom through advanced scholarship of the Word of God and God’s love for humanity.
